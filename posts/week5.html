Process Capability — Cp, Cpk and Managing Variability

📅 13 March 2026 ⏱ 6 min read 🏷 SPC · PFMEA · Process Capability

From Single Parts to Statistical Thinking

Last week I measured individual parts and found [✏️ edit: brief summary of Week 4 findings]. A single measurement, however good, tells us nothing about the consistency of the process. In a production context — particularly one where Phones-R-Us will be receiving casings from multiple suppliers — what matters is not whether one part was in tolerance, but whether the process reliably produces parts within tolerance. This distinction is the foundation of Statistical Process Control (SPC) and process capability analysis.

This week I produced a larger batch of bottom casings under identical conditions, measured the critical dimension(s) on each part, and calculated the process capability indices Cp and Cpk. These indices quantify the relationship between the natural spread of the process and the tolerance band specified on the drawing. A Cp of 1.0 means the process spread exactly fills the tolerance; a value above 1.33 is the conventional threshold for a capable process in many manufacturing contexts (Montgomery, 2019).

PFMEA — Identifying Potential Failure Modes

Before presenting the capability data, it is worth contextualising it within a Process Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (PFMEA). A PFMEA is a structured risk assessment tool that catalogues potential failure modes in a process, estimates their severity, occurrence probability, and detectability, and uses these scores to generate a Risk Priority Number (RPN). High-RPN items are targeted for preventive action.

Multi-Supplier Implications

A single-supplier capability study is informative; a multi-supplier study is essential. The core challenge articulated at the start of this module is whether casings from different suppliers — all working to the same drawing — will be sufficiently consistent to be interchangeable in the FESTO assembly line. Even if my process achieves Cpk = 1.3, a second supplier with Cpk = 0.9 introduces a fraction of non-conforming parts into the combined production stream.

This is the fundamental argument for vertical and horizontal digital integration in Industry 4.0: if each supplier shares real-time process and measurement data, defective trends can be detected and corrected before they reach the assembly stage (Thoben, Wiesner and Wuest, 2017). In the absence of such integration, the manufacturer must either accept a degree of scrap and rework or implement incoming inspection — both costly strategies.
