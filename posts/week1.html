Introduction & Blog Setup — The Phones-R-Us Challenge

📅 13 February 2026 ⏱ 4 min read 🏷 Industry 4.0 · Brief · Planning

The Brief

Welcome to my Digital Manufacturing blog for module 6E7V0013 at Manchester Metropolitan University. Over the coming weeks, this blog will serve as a living portfolio of my work, reflections, and technical discoveries as I navigate the world of additive manufacturing, metrology, and Industry 4.0 process simulation.

The central scenario for this module places me in the role of a manufacturer specialising in plastic casings through additive processes. I have been approached by Phones-R-Us, a producer of small-batch modular mobile devices, who requires both top and bottom casings for their existing handset. The contract gives me nine weeks to present validated samples that fit seamlessly with the master casings produced by a competing supplier and that can flow faultlessly through the existing FESTO CP assembly line.

The ultimate production ambition — and the key metric against which this entire body of work will be assessed — is 50,000 phones assembled per annum. This figure will drive every decision I make about printer selection, process parameters, quality thresholds, and simulation modelling.

Why This Matters — Industry 4.0 Context

The scenario is deliberately constructed to surface the central tension in modern manufacturing: the need to produce parts at scale, to tight dimensional tolerances, with predictable quality, across a distributed supplier network. As Schwab (2016) argues in his foundational account of the Fourth Industrial Revolution, the fusion of physical and digital systems — often termed cyber-physical systems — fundamentally changes the economics and capabilities of production. The challenge of interchangeable parts between suppliers is not new; it was the animating problem of the American System of Manufactures in the nineteenth century. What is new is our ability to address it with data.

In Industry 4.0 terms, achieving reliable interchangeability requires both vertical integration — connecting machines, sensors, and enterprise systems within a single factory — and horizontal integration — connecting digitally across the supply chain so that a part's dimensional data travels with it from design intent to final assembly (Lu, 2017). This blog will explore both dimensions, using the Phones-R-Us case as a concrete testbed.

Key Question for this Module

Can 3D-printed phone casings, produced across multiple machines and suppliers, be made sufficiently interchangeable to support a 50,000-unit annual production target?

My Plan for the Semester

My weekly workflow will follow three repeating activities: practical work in the lab (3D printing, scanning, FESTO observation), exploration of relevant digital manufacturing theory and literature, and reflection in these blog posts. I have set myself internal deadlines aligned with the module's recommended sub-task dates — blog live by 13 February, consistent weekly posting throughout, and final submission by 13 May 2026.
