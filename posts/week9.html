Lessons Learnt & Reflection — Digital Manufacturing in Practice

📅 13 May 2026 ⏱ 6 min read 🏷 Reflection · Industry 4.0 · Sustainability

The Journey in Summary

Nine weeks ago, I was handed a brief: produce top and bottom phone casings for Phones-R-Us using additive manufacturing, ensure they fit seamlessly with a master assembly, and demonstrate that a 50,000-unit annual production target is achievable. That brief has shaped every session in the lab, every measurement, every simulation run, and every post on this blog.

Looking back, what strikes me most is how interconnected the challenges were. Dimensional accuracy in the 3D printing process (Week 3) determined the metrology outcomes (Week 4), which in turn shaped the process capability analysis (Week 5). The reverse engineering workflow (Week 6) depended on the same measurement infrastructure. And the simulation work (Weeks 7–8) was only as reliable as the cycle time data gathered from the FESTO lab (Week 2). Digital manufacturing is not a collection of isolated tools; it is a system, and its value emerges from the connections between its parts.

Environmental and Societal Considerations

Additive manufacturing is often positioned as an inherently sustainable technology — producing parts on demand, reducing material waste through near-net-shape processes, and enabling localised production that shortens supply chains. The reality is more nuanced. FDM printing generates waste in the form of support structures and failed prints; the energy consumption of printers running continuously to meet a 50,000-unit target is non-trivial; and the polymers most commonly used (PLA, ABS, PETG) have complex end-of-life profiles (Ford and Despeisse, 2016).
