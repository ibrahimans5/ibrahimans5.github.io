Reverse Engineering the Top Casing — Scan to Print

📅 20 March 2026 ⏱ 5 min read 🏷 Reverse Engineering · 3D Scanning · CAD

The Problem — No Drawing Provided

While the bottom casing came with a full engineering drawing, Phones-R-Us provided no equivalent document for the top casing. Instead, I was given the master top casing — the physical part produced by another supplier — and tasked with manufacturing a replica that fits and functions identically. This is a reverse engineering challenge: extracting design intent from a physical artefact in order to reproduce it.

Reverse engineering is increasingly common in industrial settings. It may arise when original design documentation has been lost, when a part is being moved between suppliers, or when an organisation needs to interoperate with a third-party component for which no CAD data exists. Raja and Fernandes (2008) define reverse engineering as "the process of duplicating an existing component, subassembly, or product, without the aid of drawings, documentation, or a computer model." The digital manufacturing era has made this process substantially more tractable through 3D scanning technology.

The Scan-to-Print Workflow

The workflow I followed consists of four main stages: (1) Scan the master part to generate a point cloud or mesh, (2) Process the mesh to remove noise and fill holes, (3) Convert the mesh to a printable format (STL or direct slicer input), and (4) Print and validate the output against the master.
